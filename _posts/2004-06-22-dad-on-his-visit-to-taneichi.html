The whole object of travel is not to set foot on a foreign land; it is
at last to set foot on one's own country as a foreign land. - G. K. Chesterton

Often I feel I go to some distant region of the world to be reminded
of who I really am. - Michael Crichton

(Of course, we also wanted to check on Forrest to find out if he really
is doing fine as he routinely claims in telephone conversations.)

Summary of trip:
Getting there: The longest day of my life: Get up in Atlanta with the
sun at 6:00 AM and watch the sun set in Tokyo at about 7:00 PM. The
trick is that this is 26 hours later. We still have a few hours on
trains before we meet Forrest.
Getting home is worse, because this is the shortest night of my life.
Somehow my body prefers the extra long day to the too short night.

Three days going to three different schools. We were the lesson for
the day. "Visit by American family" We asked and answered hundreds of
questions. (Do you like miso soup?) This was great fun for all of us.


Surfing day.

Three nights on the road (more photos).




So, how is Forrest doing? Living in a nice little house in sight of
the Pacific Ocean. Surfing, baking bread, discovering all of the local
food and places to visit. Good relationships with coworkers. Loved by
students. Lots of friends, both local and English speakers.
He is a great driver, in my opinion, but is having a little trouble
convincing the Japanese authorities of that. Seems you can drive for
one year using your foreign license, but then must past a very
difficult driving test in order to be licensed for future years.

How are things different in Japan?

People are nice to each other and extremely nice to us. When the
ticket agent at the Narita Airport who was issuing our rail pass and
making seat reservations for us realized that we had only 3 minutes to
catch the train we needed in order to make the connection to get on
the last bullet train for the day she stood up, told us to follow, and
ran to hold the train for us. We didn't know anyone could run that
fast in high heels.


Life is lived close to the floor. Sleeping on futons and eating meals
while sitting on the floor is routine. (Next time I will practice in
advance.)

You really do not wear your outdoor shoes inside.

Lots of rice and fish. Gardens in every possible place.
Tiny cars. Clothes dried outdoors.

Based on my observation at the three schools in Japan that I visited
it is clear that we have omitted a few things from the Mountain
Community School plans:


  • A place to store shoes near the front door where you remove your outdoor shoes and put on your indoor shoes.
  • Sinks in the hall for tooth brushing after lunch.
  • An art room, music room, International Understanding Classroom, computer room, gymnasium, lunch room, auditorium, swimming pool, and tennis courts. Nurses room.


Not sure why they had a lunchroom as the meals were served by students
in the classrooms.

Also, the schools are cleaned by the students after classes.

At one school we participated in the annual cleaning of the swimming pool.
This was a cooperative event where every student and staff member
scrubbed and cleaned the pool to prepare for filling. This was
accomplished in one hour with not a bit of instruction required nor a
single complaint registered.
